## Case Study: Exploring Global Video Game Sales with Agentic EDA

To demonstrate the full analytical workflow enabled by our system, we present a case study using the Video Game Sales dataset (vgsales.csv), which contains 16,598 records of video game titles with attributes including platform, genre, publisher, release year, and regional sales figures (North America, Europe, Japan, and other regions). We invited an analyst, P1, a data science researcher with five years of experience, to explore this dataset using Agentic EDA. P1's goal was to uncover the structural dynamics of the global video game market — identifying which factors drive commercial success across regions and how the industry has evolved over time. The exploration unfolded in five phases over approximately 35 minutes, during which the system autonomously executed 15 analysis tasks in serial and extracted 42 atomic insights spanning 9 of the 11 taxonomy types.

### Phase 1: Orientation and Autonomous Planning

P1 began by selecting a completed run from the **Run Browser**, which lists all exploration runs with status badges, step counts, and insight tallies. Entering the workspace, the three-panel layout immediately oriented P1: the **Topics** panel on the left, the **Exploration Graph** in the center, and the **Inspector** on the right. P1 clicked the table icon in the header bar to open the **DataView** modal — a paginated preview of the raw dataset with sticky column headers and row numbers — confirming that `NA_Sales`, `EU_Sales`, `JP_Sales`, and `Other_Sales` represent regional revenue in millions of USD, and that `Year` contains release years ranging from 1980 to 2020.

The system's **Planner** had autonomously generated an initial set of five analysis plans based on the dataset schema and statistical profile. These plans covered broad analytical angles: overall sales distributions, top publishers by revenue, genre popularity trends, platform market share, and regional sales comparisons. P1 observed these plans in the **Topics** panel's "Overview" tab — a compact nested tree where each node displayed a colored status dot and a short label. Switching to the "Detailed" tab revealed the full plan text and motivation for each task. In the center panel, the **Exploration Graph** rendered these initial plans as dashed-border cards radiating outward from the central root node — a dark circle labeled with the dataset filename — along the first depth ring of the radial layout.

*"I didn't have to think about where to start,"* P1 noted. *"The system proposed exactly the kind of broad survey I would have done manually, but it also included angles I might have deprioritized, like data quality checks."*

### Phase 2: Live Monitoring of Serial Execution

The **Dispatcher**, configured with the default breadth-first (BFS) strategy, selected the first plan by shallowest depth and earliest creation time. P1 watched the analysis unfold in real time through the **Inspector** panel. Clicking a running plan — indicated by a pulsing green line and a status-labeled card on the graph — revealed the agent's full multi-turn reasoning process streamed via SSE.

The Inspector's **Plan Stream** displayed the agent's iterative workflow: the LLM first called `reflect_on_results` to articulate its analytical approach, then issued an `execute_code` call producing a histogram of `Global_Sales` with a log-transformed axis. The streaming view rendered each code block syntax-highlighted, stdout in monospace, and plot images inline. After observing the output, the agent reflected on the extreme right skew, then executed a second code block computing percentile breakdowns and generating a box plot. The system enforced a minimum of two successful code executions before allowing the agent to call `complete_analysis`, ensuring substantive evidence for every task.

When the agent encountered a minor error — attempting to call `.describe()` on a filtered subset that was unexpectedly empty — the streaming error block rendered the traceback in red, and the agent autonomously corrected its filter logic in the next turn. P1 could follow this self-healing behavior in real time through the plan stream. The attempt indicator at the top of the stream showed "Attempt 1," confirming the agent recovered within a single attempt rather than requiring a retry.

The **Exploration Graph** updated dynamically as each serial task progressed: the active plan's card pulsed green, and upon completion, its line solidified to a dark stroke while a **Summary Card** replaced the plan card — a white rounded rectangle displaying the insight's short label, a count badge for atomic insights, and a row of taxonomy-colored dots showing the distribution of insight types. Failed plans turned red, clearly distinguishable from successful branches. P1 appreciated this at-a-glance status encoding: *"I could see the health of the entire exploration without reading any text — solid lines with cards meant productive analysis, red meant something went wrong."*

### Phase 3: Insight Crystallization and Taxonomy Mapping

As the first wave of analyses completed, the **Summarizer** extracted structured atomic insights from each execution's output. The Summarizer consumed multimodal evidence — code, stdout, and plot images — and produced typed `AtomicInsight` objects, each validated against the dataset schema and the 11-type taxonomy. Insights with unknown taxonomy types, unmapped column references, or missing plot evidence were automatically filtered out, ensuring data quality in the exploration tree.

The **Coverage Map** in the lower-left panel began to populate, revealing the analytical terrain covered so far. This heatmap, with the 11 taxonomy types as rows and dataset columns as columns, used a blue intensity gradient to encode how many atomic insights existed for each (type, column) pair. A legend bar at the top showed the count scale from zero to the current maximum. After the first four tasks completed, P1 noticed that the `Global_Sales` column was heavily covered across `distribution`, `extreme`, and `value` types, while `JP_Sales` and `Other_Sales` remained almost entirely blank — a clear signal of under-explored territory.

P1 clicked on the (`rank`, `Publisher`) cell in the Coverage Map, which had an intensity of 3. The **Inspector** immediately switched to **Coverage Cell View**, displaying three atomic insights: "Nintendo leads all publishers with cumulative global sales of 1,786.56M USD," "The top 5 publishers (Nintendo, Electronic Arts, Activision, Sony, Ubisoft) account for 38.2% of total market revenue," and "The publisher concentration follows a power-law distribution with a Gini coefficient of 0.847." Each insight appeared as an **Atomic Insight Card** showing its taxonomy type badge (color-coded), referenced column tags, the insight text, and a click-to-zoom plot image. Expanding the collapsible "Code" and "Output" sections revealed the underlying Python code and execution output, lazy-loaded from the artifact store.

The **Topics** panel's "Overview" tab now showed a growing tree structure. Completed nodes displayed green status dots, while pending drilldown plans showed gray dots. P1 could click any node to drive the Inspector, creating a fluid workflow between the tree overview and the detail view. *"The Topics tree gave me a table of contents for the entire exploration,"* P1 remarked. *"I could scan the short labels to find what I was looking for, then click through to the full evidence."*

### Phase 4: Strategic Steering and Autonomous Drill-Down

By this point, seven of the initial and early drilldown plans had completed, producing 21 atomic insights. The system had already begun **autonomous drill-down**: after extracting insights from the "Genre Popularity Trends" analysis (which revealed that Action games dominated in volume but Sports games had higher median sales), the **Planner** automatically proposed up to three child plans — investigating the Action genre's regional breakdown, comparing Sports vs. Action sales trajectories over time, and examining whether genre dominance varied by platform. These drill-down plans appeared on the graph as new branches extending from the parent Summary Card to the second depth ring, with the planner providing multimodal context by loading plot images from the parent insight to inform its proposals.

The **embedding-based deduplication** silently prevented redundant exploration. When the drill-down planner proposed a plan to "analyze sales by genre across regions," the system computed cosine similarity against all existing plan and insight embeddings, detected a score of 0.93 (above the 0.92 threshold) with an existing plan "regional sales breakdown by game genre," and automatically filtered it out. P1 later noticed this efficiency in the event log: *"Without deduplication, I've seen LLM-based systems waste half their budget re-asking the same question in slightly different words."*

P1 noticed that the system's default BFS strategy was producing broad but shallow coverage. Wanting to pursue a promising lead about Japan's distinctive market, P1 used the **strategy toggle** in the workspace header bar to switch from BFS to Greedy mid-exploration. The switch was immediate: the **Dispatcher** reordered the frontier to prioritize plans with the highest impact scores, regardless of depth. An `exploration_strategy_changed` event appeared in the SSE stream, and the graph's growth pattern visibly shifted — deeper second- and third-ring branches began extending from the most insight-rich Summary Cards, rather than new first-ring nodes.

*"Switching to Greedy was a turning point,"* P1 reflected. *"The system had been covering breadth systematically, but I wanted to chase the Japan market thread. One click changed the exploration's direction without interrupting anything."*

The **impact scoring** mechanism also became visible at this stage. P1 selected pending plans in the **Topics** panel and observed their priority scores in the Inspector. A plan to analyze "Japanese market preferences by genre" had an impact score of 0.31, computed from the ratio of `JP_Sales` magnitude under the plan's filters to total global sales. In contrast, a drill-down plan to further decompose Nintendo's platform-specific sales had a score of 0.12 — lower because the filtered subset was narrower. The impact scores grounded the scheduling in data characteristics rather than arbitrary ordering, and P1 found this transparency valuable for understanding why the system chose to explore certain directions.

### Phase 5: Deep Exploration, Synthesis, and Coordinated Navigation

After the Greedy switch, the exploration tree grew to three depth levels. The ego-centric **Exploration Graph** now displayed a rich radial structure: the root dataset node at center, first-ring Summary Cards for the initial broad analyses, second-ring cards for drill-downs, and third-ring cards for the deepest investigations. P1 used the graph's pan and zoom controls — dragging to pan, scrolling to zoom, with a percentage indicator in the bottom-right corner — to navigate the growing tree. Clicking any Summary Card triggered **focus highlighting**: the selected node and its entire path back to root glowed blue, while non-path elements dimmed, allowing P1 to trace complete analytical narratives through the radial structure.

A particularly rich branch emerged from the "Regional Sales Comparison" analysis. The initial task had discovered that Japan's market preferences diverge sharply from Western markets. The first drill-down, "Japanese RPG Dominance Analysis," revealed that Role-Playing games accounted for 52.3% of Japanese sales versus only 12.8% in North America — a `difference`-type insight with a striking divergence. A second drill-down investigated the Western market correlation, uncovering that the correlation between `NA_Sales` and `EU_Sales` (r=0.77) was significantly stronger than between `NA_Sales` and `JP_Sales` (r=0.41), suggesting Western markets share similar consumer preferences — an `association`-type insight. P1 traced this three-level chain by clicking through the graph's highlighted path, reading each Summary Card's label and taxonomy dots to understand the analytical progression before diving into the Inspector for full evidence.

The "Detailed" tab in the **Topics** panel proved essential for understanding the tree's logical structure. Each node rendered as a paired plan-and-summary card: the plan card showed the task's status icon, depth level, and full analytical question, while the summary card below it displayed the summarizer's 2-3 sentence overview. Children were indented with left border lines, creating a visual hierarchy that mirrored the exploration's logical progression. P1 developed a workflow of scanning the Topics tree for structural context, then clicking through to the graph for spatial orientation, and finally reading the Inspector for full evidence chains.

The **Coverage Map** now told a compelling completeness story. After 15 tasks and 42 atomic insights, `distribution` and `value` types were well-covered across most numeric columns, `trend` insights concentrated on `Year`-related analyses, `association` insights bridged regional sales columns, and `rank` insights clustered around `Publisher` and `Genre`. Two cells remained empty — (`cluster`, `Platform`) and (`data_quality`, `Year`) — which P1 noted as potential directions for future exploration. The heatmap's blue intensity gradient made coverage gaps immediately visible, serving as both a progress indicator and a guide for what remained unexplored.

P1 used the **bookmark** feature to flag the most significant findings. By toggling the bookmark icon in the Inspector's summary view, P1 marked five key insights: the power-law publisher concentration (Gini=0.847), Japan's RPG dominance (52.3% market share), the Western market correlation (r=0.77), a console lifecycle S-curve pattern discovered in the platform analysis branch, and a `data_quality` insight revealing that 271 records had missing `Year` values concentrated in titles from smaller publishers. These bookmarks persisted across view switches, allowing P1 to quickly revisit critical findings.

The **multi-view coordinated selection** proved essential during synthesis. All three panels — Topics, Graph, and Inspector — responded to the same selection state. Clicking a node in the Topics tree highlighted it on the graph and loaded its details in the Inspector; clicking a Summary Card on the graph scrolled the Topics tree and drove the Inspector content; clicking a Coverage Map cell filtered the Inspector to matching atomic insights. *"Each view answered a different question about the same insight,"* P1 explained. *"The Topics tree showed me the logical structure, the graph showed me the spatial shape of the exploration, the Coverage Map showed me what's left to explore, and the Inspector showed me the actual evidence. I never felt lost because I could always ground myself in whichever perspective I needed."*

### Findings and Analyst Feedback

Through this 35-minute exploration, P1 uncovered several non-obvious findings that would have required significant manual effort to discover: (1) the extreme publisher concentration following a power-law distribution, (2) Japan's structurally distinct market preferences diverging from a correlated Western bloc, (3) a universal console lifecycle S-curve with region-specific adoption timing, and (4) a data quality issue with systematically missing year values biased toward smaller publishers — a potential confound for any temporal analysis.

P1 highlighted three aspects of the system that most enhanced the analytical experience. First, the **autonomous drill-down** mechanism eliminated the "what next?" friction that typically interrupts exploratory analysis: *"Every time I finished reading one insight, the system had already proposed follow-up questions. I spent my time evaluating findings instead of formulating queries."* Second, the **three-panel coordinated navigation** enabled rapid context-switching between structural, spatial, and evidential perspectives on the same exploration: *"The Topics tree, the radial graph, and the Inspector each gave me a different lens on the same data. Clicking in one view updated the others instantly — I never had to manually synchronize my mental model."* Third, the **live strategy switching** gave P1 a sense of agency over an otherwise autonomous process: *"It's the right balance — the system drives, but I can steer. Switching from BFS to Greedy felt like adjusting the zoom level on a microscope."*

P1 also identified areas for improvement: the Coverage Map's intensity-only encoding could benefit from a quality dimension beyond count; the Topics panel became dense with more than 12 completed tasks, suggesting the need for semantic grouping or filtering; and the ego-centric graph's radial layout occasionally produced overlapping cards at higher depths despite the collision resolution algorithm, making it harder to distinguish closely related branches. These observations inform our future work directions discussed in Section 7.
